## 1. COMPANY OVERVIEW & RISK PROFILE

### 1.1 Corporate Identity

Bharat Steel & Alloys Ltd. is a mid-to-large integrated steel producer operating two manufacturing facilities in India — a primary BF-BOF complex in Raipur, Chhattisgarh (3.2 MTPA installed capacity) and a rolling mill in Visakhapatnam, Andhra Pradesh (0.8 MTPA). For FY2024, BSAL reported revenue of approximately $620M (₹5,160 Cr) with an EBITDA margin of approximately 20% ($124M). Total fixed assets stand at $380M. The company employs 7,400 people across its two production sites and a Mumbai corporate office.

BSAL is primarily a domestic steel producer with 22% export exposure — primarily to UAE (12%), Bangladesh (7%), and Sri Lanka (3%). ### 1.3 Sectoral Context

Steel is among the hardest-to-abate industrial sectors globally, accounting for approximately 7–9% of global CO2 emissions. India is the world's second-largest steel producer and is expected to more than double its steel demand by 2050. This creates a structural tension: massive demand growth on one side, and mounting decarbonization pressure on the other.

For Indian integrated steel producers specifically, the near-term regulatory landscape is intensifying: SEBI's BRSR Core mandate (operative from FY24 for top 150 listed companies), the Bureau of Energy Efficiency's PAT Scheme Cycle 6, and the emerging India Carbon Market under the Energy Conservation Amendment Act 2022 all create direct compliance and cost implications for companies like BSAL.

The global steel transition is also accelerating. Green steel (produced via hydrogen-based DRI or electric arc furnace with renewable electricity) is beginning to command price premiums in European and Japanese markets. While these markets represent a small share of BSAL's current customer base, the trajectory is clear: by 2030–35, brown steel (BF-BOF produced) will face structural market access constraints in premium export destinations.

BSAL's current technology — BF-BOF — is the most carbon-intensive primary steelmaking route. Transitioning away from it requires either significant CAPEX investment (EAF route) or a hydrogen-based DRI retrofit — both requiring multi-decade planning horizons and capital commitments that are not visible in the current disclosure.

### 5.2 Water Stress — Critical Exposure

**This is the most pressing physical risk for BSAL.**

Raipur, Chhattisgarh receives a WRI Aqueduct score of 3.8/5 (High water stress). Integrated steel manufacturing is highly water-intensive — BSAL uses water for blast furnace cooling, coke quenching, dust suppression, and continuous casting. The process is not discretionary.

62% of BSAL's manufacturing capacity is located in water-stressed zones. The WRI projects this score to worsen under 3°C+ scenarios as the Mahanadi basin — which supplies much of central India's industrial water — faces increasing seasonal variability and groundwater depletion.

BSAL's Zero Liquid Discharge system at the Raipur site is a genuine mitigant — it reduces fresh water consumption by recirculating process water, and the CPCB certificate provides verification. This is why the water resilience factor is 0.65 (not the worst possible 1.0). However, the ZLD system addresses liquid discharge, not raw water procurement risk. If the WRI Aqueduct score at Raipur deteriorates to 4.5+ (Extremely High), BSAL faces either rationing-driven production curtailments or significantly higher water procurement costs from distant sources.

**No formal water risk assessment or water stewardship strategy was found in any uploaded document.** This is a gap that would fail any CDP Water Security questionnaire.

### 5.3 Heat Stress — Operational and Labor Risk

Raipur is one of India's hottest cities. The 95th percentile Wet Bulb Globe Temperature (WBGT) during peak summer months currently reaches 29.8°C. For BSAL's 5,200 heavy-work employees at the Raipur site, this is already at the threshold of significant productivity impairment (heavy work threshold: 23°C WBGT per Kjellstrom 2016 model).

Under IPCC AR6 projections for a 3°C scenario, Raipur's WBGT p95 increases to approximately 32.2°C by 2050. At this level, outdoor heavy work would be near-prohibited during peak months — the Kjellstrom model projects approximately 28% productivity loss for heavy outdoor work at 32°C WBGT. This translates to significant labor cost inflation and potential production constraints during Q1 of each year.

No heat stress mitigation CAPEX was identified in BSAL's FY2024 investment disclosures — no cooling infrastructure upgrades, no shift scheduling adaptations, no WBGT monitoring program. The absence of heat stress planning at a site already operating near the threshold is a material operational risk gap.

### 5.4 Coastal Risk — Vizag Site

The Vizag rolling mill sits 4.2km from the Bay of Bengal. The Bay of Bengal experiences among the highest cyclone intensity in the Indian Ocean region. IPCC AR6 projects that while cyclone frequency may stabilize, peak intensity (wind speeds, storm surge) is projected to increase under 2–4°C scenarios.

Beyond cyclone risk, the 4.2km proximity places the Vizag site within the potential inundation zone of IPCC SLR projections under high-end scenarios (RCP 8.5 / SSP5-8.5: median SLR 0.63–1.01m by 2100, with tails up to 2m+).

At 18% of total fixed assets, the Vizag site represents $68.4M of asset value with partially unhedged coastal risk. No coastal risk assessment, cyclone BCDR plan, or parametric insurance coverage was evidenced.

Transition risk is where climate change becomes a P&L and balance sheet issue in the near term. The carbon pricing, energy cost, and technology lock-in components are not abstract — they represent identifiable cash flow impacts within the next investment cycle.

### 6.2 Carbon Pricing Exposure

India's carbon pricing landscape is evolving rapidly. The Energy Conservation Amendment Act 2022 provides the legal basis for the India Carbon Market (ICM), with trading expected to commence in 2026–2027. BSAL's current PAT Scheme position is adequate — it achieved its Cycle 5 target. However, Cycle 6 (FY2024–2026) includes tighter benchmarks, and the transition to the ICM will introduce a market-priced carbon cost that replaces the PAT certificate trading mechanism.

### 6.3 Energy Cost Exposure

BSAL's energy intensity of 23.4 GJ/t is 18.2% above the sector median (19.8 GJ/t). Annual energy cost is approximately $205M — 33% of revenue. This is an outsized energy cost relative to peers.

Grid decarbonization will initially increase electricity tariffs as utilities retire coal generation and build renewable capacity. In a Disorderly Transition, this transition is abrupt and unmanaged, driving grid electricity premiums of 18–42% by 2030–2040. Given BSAL's high electricity consumption (3,800 GWh/year), even a 10% energy cost increase represents $20.5M of annual OPEX inflation.

BSAL's current renewable energy share of 4.2% is below the sector median of 8.1% and far below the best-in-class 35%+. There is no renewable energy procurement strategy (PPAs, captive renewable, RECs program) documented in any disclosure.

### 6.4 Technology Lock-In

This is the most structurally significant transition risk for BSAL.

BF-BOF steelmaking requires coke (produced from coking coal) and is irreducibly carbon-intensive at current technology. The equipment has an operational life of 20–25 years. BSAL's Raipur blast furnace was most recently refurbished in 2019 — suggesting remaining asset life to approximately 2039–2044.

The global trajectory for steel decarbonization points to two viable pathways: (1) Electric Arc Furnace (EAF) using scrap steel, and (2) Hydrogen-based Direct Reduced Iron (DRI-H2). Both require substantial capital investment and are only viable with access to affordable renewable electricity or green hydrogen.

BSAL has disclosed no investment in either pathway. Its current CAPEX program — ₹820 Cr in FY2024 — appears to be entirely focused on capacity maintenance and efficiency improvements within the existing BF-BOF route. This is rational in the short term but creates compounding lock-in risk. Each year of BF-BOF investment without a technology transition plan represents an additional year of carbon-intensive asset life that must eventually be stranded or retrofitted.

## 7. SUPPLY CHAIN

**Score: 19.3 / 100 — D | 12th Percentile | LAGGARD**

### 7.1 Supply Chain Risk Profile

BSAL's supply chain has three structural vulnerabilities from a climate risk perspective: coking coal geographic concentration, iron ore water risk exposure, and near-total absence of supplier climate risk monitoring.

**Coking Coal Concentration**
78% of BSAL's coking coal (1.8 MTPA annually) is sourced from Australia. Australia's Bowen Basin — the primary coking coal producing region — faces increasing cyclone and flood risk that could disrupt exports. Queensland flood events in 2010–11 and 2021–22 caused significant coking coal export disruptions globally. As Australia's climate risk increases and export infrastructure faces more frequent weather-related shutdowns, single-country concentration of 78% for a CRITICAL input material represents unmanaged supply chain risk.

**Iron Ore Water Risk**
Iron ore is sourced from Odisha (78% of supplier base). Odisha's Keonjhar and Sundargarh districts — India's primary iron ore producing regions — face increasing cyclone exposure (Odisha coast) and water stress affecting ore processing operations. No supplier climate risk assessment for iron ore suppliers was evidenced.

**Supplier ESG Coverage**

### 12.3 Scenario Analysis Narrative

**Orderly Transition (Net Zero 2050):**
Even in the most favorable scenario, BSAL faces $93.8M in combined risk by 2030 (15.1% of revenue). The key driver is carbon pricing ($48.2M at $67/tCO2e). This scenario is the one where early action is rewarded: if BSAL achieves SBTi validation and begins technology transition investment, the stranded asset risk is manageable and the 2050 exposure plateaus at approximately $362M — painful but survivable at much higher revenue levels.

**Disorderly Transition (~3°C):**
This is the baseline scenario for the risk simulation. BSAL faces $148M by 2030 (23.9%) and the exposure is non-linear — $451M by 2040 and $911M by 2050. The non-linearity reflects carbon pricing compounding on large absolute emissions plus growing physical risk. In this scenario, BSAL's survival as an independent company depends on technology transition investment beginning by 2028 at the latest.

**Hot House (~4°C):**
The Hot House scenario is counterintuitively less financially damaging in the near term because carbon prices remain low (governments fail to implement meaningful carbon pricing). The financial exposure by 2030 is only $59.5M. However, the physical risk trajectory is severe: $152M of physical risk alone by 2050, driven by extreme heat events and water scarcity. In this scenario, the Raipur site faces potential operational viability challenges from water stress and heat by the 2040s, irrespective of any financial policy response.

### 12.4 Key Simulation Insight

**The most financially dangerous scenario for BSAL is Disorderly Transition** — not because of physical risk but because it combines moderate-to-high carbon pricing (enough to be painful but not early enough for BSAL to adapt) with increasing physical risk and technology lock-in penalties. In an orderly scenario, BSAL has time to plan. In a hot house scenario, carbon prices are low. In the disorderly scenario, prices rise faster than the company can respond and physical risk simultaneously intensifies.
