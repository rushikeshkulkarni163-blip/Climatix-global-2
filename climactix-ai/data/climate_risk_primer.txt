SECTION 1: TRANSITION RISK — DEFINITION AND TAXONOMY
------------------------------------------------------
Transition risk refers to the financial and business risks arising from the global shift toward a low-carbon economy. The TCFD (Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures) categorizes transition risk into four sub-types:

1. Policy and Legal Risk: Governments are introducing carbon pricing mechanisms, emissions trading schemes (ETS), and sector-specific regulations. The EU Emissions Trading System (EU ETS) covers approximately 40% of EU greenhouse gas emissions. Carbon prices in the EU ETS reached over €100/tCO2 in 2023. Companies exposed to carbon-intensive operations face direct cost increases as carbon prices rise toward $130-250/tCO2 by 2050 under NGFS Net Zero scenarios.

2. Technology Risk: The rapid advancement of renewable energy, battery storage, electric vehicles, and green hydrogen is disrupting incumbent fossil fuel industries. Solar PV costs have fallen 89% since 2010. Wind power costs have fallen 70%. EV battery costs have dropped from $1,100/kWh in 2010 to below $120/kWh in 2023. Companies that fail to adapt face technology obsolescence.

3. Market Risk: Consumer preferences and institutional investor mandates are shifting toward sustainable products and services. Over $40 trillion in assets under management now incorporate ESG criteria. Demand for fossil fuels is projected to peak before 2030 under IEA Net Zero 2050 scenario. Oil demand falls from 100 mb/d in 2019 to 24 mb/d by 2050 under this scenario.

4. Reputational Risk: Companies perceived as laggards on climate action face growing pressure from investors, customers, regulators, and civil society. ESG ratings from MSCI, Sustainalytics, and CDP increasingly influence institutional investment decisions and cost of capital.


SECTION 2: PHYSICAL CLIMATE RISK
----------------------------------
Physical climate risk refers to the financial risks from the physical effects of climate change. The IPCC AR6 (2021-2022) identifies two categories:

Acute Physical Risk: Event-driven risks from increased severity and frequency of extreme weather events:
- Tropical cyclones: intensification expected under 1.5°C and 2°C scenarios
- Flooding: 100-year floods may become 10-20 year events under 2°C warming
- Wildfires: area burned in high-risk regions projected to increase 50-100%
- Extreme heat events: frequency increasing 4-5x under 2°C warming

Chronic Physical Risk: Longer-term shifts in climate patterns:
- Sea level rise: 0.3-1.0m by 2100 under SSP2-4.5; up to 2m under SSP5-8.5
- Chronic heat stress: Wet bulb temperatures exceeding 35°C (survivability limit) in tropical regions
- Water stress: 2-3 billion people projected to face water scarcity by 2050
- Agricultural disruption: Crop yield reductions of 2-6% per decade in major growing regions

Financial materiality of physical risk:
- Real estate: Coastal properties face devaluation as flood and sea level risk increases
- Infrastructure: Energy, transport, and water infrastructure require climate-proofing
- Insurance: Increasing uninsurability of high-risk assets; premiums rising 10-30% annually in high-risk regions
- Agriculture and food: Supply chain disruption affecting food companies and commodity traders


SECTION 3: TCFD FRAMEWORK
---------------------------
The Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures (TCFD) was established by the Financial Stability Board (FSB) in 2015. Its 2017 recommendations have become the global standard for climate risk disclosure.

Four Core Pillars:

GOVERNANCE: Board oversight of climate-related risks and opportunities. Management's role in assessing and managing climate-related risks.

STRATEGY: Actual and potential impacts of climate-related risks and opportunities on the organization's businesses, strategy, and financial planning. Scenario analysis required to assess resilience.

RISK MANAGEMENT: Processes for identifying, assessing, and managing climate-related risks. Integration into overall enterprise risk management.

METRICS AND TARGETS: Metrics used to assess climate-related risks and opportunities. Scope 1, 2, and 3 GHG emissions disclosed. Targets used to manage climate-related risks.

Scenario Analysis (TCFD requirement):
Companies are required to assess their strategic resilience under different climate scenarios, including:
- 2°C scenario (Paris Agreement minimum)
- Well below 2°C / 1.5°C scenario (Paris Agreement ambition)
- Current policies scenario (business as usual)

Key scenarios used: IEA NZE 2050, NGFS Net Zero 2050, NGFS Current Policies, IPCC SSP1-1.9, IPCC SSP2-4.5, IPCC SSP5-8.5.


SECTION 4: CSRD — EU CORPORATE SUSTAINABILITY REPORTING DIRECTIVE
------------------------------------------------------------------
The Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) is EU legislation that entered into force in January 2023, replacing the previous Non-Financial Reporting Directive (NFRD).

Scope: Approximately 50,000 companies are required to report, compared to 11,700 under NFRD.

Phased timeline:
- FY2024 reporting (due 2025): Large EU companies already subject to NFRD
- FY2025 reporting (due 2026): All large EU companies (500+ employees OR €40M+ revenue OR €20M+ assets)
- FY2026 reporting (due 2027): Listed SMEs (with opt-out until 2028)
- FY2028 reporting (due 2029): Non-EU companies with €150M+ EU net turnover

Double Materiality Assessment:
CSRD introduces mandatory double materiality — companies must assess:
1. Financial materiality: How climate and ESG risks affect the company's financial performance
2. Impact materiality: How the company's activities impact the environment and society

ESRS E1 (Climate Change): The most detailed standard. Requires:
- Transition plan aligned with 1.5°C
- Scope 1, 2, and 3 GHG emissions disclosure
- Physical and transition risk assessment
- Climate-related targets and progress
- Carbon offsets and carbon removal disclosure


SECTION 5: CARBON MARKETS
--------------------------
Carbon markets put a price on greenhouse gas emissions to incentivize reduction.

Compliance Markets (Mandatory):
- EU ETS: World's largest carbon market. Covers power, industry, aviation, shipping. Phase 4 (2021-2030) tightens cap by 4.3% annually. Price: €50-100+/tCO2.
- UK ETS: Post-Brexit UK market. Linked tightly to EU ETS trajectory.
- California Cap-and-Trade: Covers 85% of CA GHG emissions. Price: $30-40/tCO2.
- China ETS: World's largest by coverage. Power sector only in Phase 1. Price: $8-10/tCO2 (early phase).

Voluntary Carbon Markets (VCM):
- Companies purchase carbon credits to offset emissions beyond regulatory requirements
- Credit types: Avoidance (preventing emissions) vs. Removal (capturing CO2)
- Standards: Verra VCS, Gold Standard, American Carbon Registry, Climate Action Reserve
- VCM credit prices range from $3-300/tCO2 depending on quality and type
- High-quality removal credits (direct air capture, enhanced weathering) command premium prices

Science Based Targets and Carbon Markets:
SBTi's Corporate Net-Zero Standard allows residual emissions offset only after achieving 90-95% absolute reduction. Carbon credits cannot substitute for genuine emissions reductions in SBTi-aligned targets.


SECTION 6: ESG RATINGS AND FRAMEWORKS
---------------------------------------
ESG ratings assess companies' Environmental, Social, and Governance performance.

Major ESG Rating Providers:
- MSCI ESG Ratings: AAA to CCC scale. Used by institutional investors globally.
- Sustainalytics: Risk rating scale (negligible to severe). Focuses on unmanaged ESG risk.
- CDP: A-F scale for climate (formerly Carbon Disclosure Project). Focuses on disclosure quality and action.
- ISS ESG: Corporate rating for proxy advisory and engagement.
- S&P Global CSA: Inputs to Dow Jones Sustainability Indices.

Key divergence issue: ESG ratings from different providers often diverge significantly. Studies show average correlation of only 0.54 between major ESG rating providers, compared to 0.99 for credit ratings. This reflects different methodologies, scope, and weighting of ESG factors.

SFDR Product Classification:
Under EU SFDR, financial products are classified as:
- Article 6: No ESG integration (conventional)
- Article 8: Promotes environmental or social characteristics
- Article 9: Has sustainable investment as its objective (highest standard)

Article 9 funds must demonstrate all investments are "sustainable investments" as defined by SFDR, with DNSH (Do No Significant Harm) compliance across all six environmental objectives of the EU Taxonomy.


SECTION 7: NET ZERO AND SCIENCE-BASED TARGETS
----------------------------------------------
Net Zero refers to achieving a balance between greenhouse gas emissions produced and removed from the atmosphere.

IPCC Carbon Budget:
The remaining carbon budget for 1.5°C is approximately 500 GtCO2 (as of 2020), consistent with 50% probability. At current emission rates (~40 GtCO2/year), this budget is exhausted in approximately 12 years.

Corporate Net-Zero (SBTi Standard):
1. Near-term targets: 50%+ reduction in Scope 1+2+3 emissions by 2030 (from 2020 baseline)
2. Long-term targets: 90-95% absolute reduction by 2050
3. Residual emissions: Neutralized by permanent carbon removal (not avoidance offsets)

Key Net-Zero milestones by sector (IEA NZE 2050):
- Power sector: Net zero by 2035 in advanced economies; globally by 2040
- Road transport: No new ICE car sales after 2035 (EU), 2030 (leading markets)
- Steel: Near-zero emission steelmaking representing 50% of production by 2050
- Aviation: Sustainable Aviation Fuel (SAF) providing 40% of fuel by 2050
- Buildings: Zero-carbon-ready buildings representing 50% of new builds by 2030


SECTION 8: CLIMATE FINANCE
----------------------------
Climate finance refers to financial flows directed toward climate change mitigation and adaptation.

Green Bonds:
Fixed income instruments where proceeds are exclusively applied to green projects. The Green Bond Principles (ICMA) provide the voluntary framework. $500+ billion in green bonds issued annually as of 2023. EU Green Bond Standard (EUGBS) requires alignment with EU Taxonomy.

Sustainability-Linked Bonds (SLBs):
Bonds with financial characteristics (e.g., coupon step-up) linked to sustainability performance targets. Unlike green bonds, proceeds are not restricted. Key metrics: GHG emissions intensity, renewable energy share, SBTi validation.

Transition Finance:
Financing for hard-to-abate sectors undertaking credible decarbonization pathways. Controversial area — risk of greenwashing. Singapore Taxonomy and ASEAN Taxonomy include transition category.

Blended Finance:
Combination of concessional public finance with private finance to de-risk climate investments in emerging markets. Climate Policy Initiative estimates $4.3 trillion/year needed by 2030; current flows at ~$1 trillion.

Climate Risk Integration in Lending:
Central banks (ECB, Bank of England, PBOC) and financial regulators increasingly require banks to:
- Conduct climate stress testing (NGFS scenarios)
- Disclose climate-related credit risk in loan portfolios
- Integrate climate risk into credit rating methodologies
